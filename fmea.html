What is Failure Mode & Effects Analysis (FMEA)?

Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) is a systematic, proactive method for evaluating a process or design to identify where and how it might fail and to assess the relative impact of different failures. The goal is to identify, prioritize, and eliminate or reduce potential failures, starting with the highest-priority ones. This tool supports the modern AIAG & VDA 7-Step FMEA process, focusing on the Structure, Function, and Failure Analysis steps.

Key components of an FMEA include:

  • Failure Modes: The specific ways in which a component, system, or process could fail to meet the design intent.
  • Effects of Failure: The consequences of a failure mode on the system, customer, or downstream processes.
  • Causes of Failure: The design or process weaknesses, errors, or conditions that could trigger the failure mode.
  • Risk Priority Number (RPN): A numerical ranking of the risk associated with each potential failure. It is calculated as: RPN = Severity (S) × Occurrence (O) × Detection (D)
    • Severity (S): The seriousness of the effect of the failure.
    • Occurrence (O): The likelihood that the cause of the failure will occur.
    • Detection (D): The ability of current controls to detect the cause or the failure mode before it reaches the customer.
  • Action Priority (AP): A modern AIAG‑VDA approach that classifies risk as High (H), Medium (M) or Low (L) based on logical combinations of S, O and D. It avoids the limitations of RPN (e.g., 10×8×1 = 80 vs 4×5×4 = 80) and focuses on the most critical risks first. This tool calculates AP automatically for each failure mode and for the post‑action state (AP2).

Brief History of FMEA and Modern Standards

The FMEA methodology was first developed by the U.S. military in the late 1940s and documented in military procedure MIL-P-1629. It was designed to evaluate the reliability and safety of military equipment by identifying all possible failure modes and their effects on mission success.

Its value was quickly recognized by the aerospace industry. NASA extensively used FMEA during the Apollo program in the 1960s to mitigate risks and enhance the safety of space missions. In the 1970s, the automotive industry, led by Ford Motor Company, adopted FMEA to improve vehicle safety and quality, popularizing its use in the commercial sector. Today, the standard for FMEA is often governed by the AIAG & VDA FMEA Handbook, which promotes a 7-Step approach for systematic analysis and risk mitigation. This tool helps practitioners structure their analysis according to these modern principles, including the latest Action Priority (AP) tables and post‑action risk assessment (S2, O2, D2, RPN2, AP2).

How to Use This Tool: The 7-Step FMEA Approach (AIAG‑VDA)

  1. Step 1 – Planning & Preparation: Fill in the "Planning" tab with project name, team, date, FMEA type (DFMEA/PFMEA) and customer information. This sets the scope and context.
  2. Step 2 – Structure Analysis: In the "Structure" tab, add all components (parts or process steps). Mark external elements (user, power source, etc.). Define contacts (interfaces) between components using the "Contacts" panel.
  3. Step 3 – Function Analysis: In the "Functions" panel, describe what the system is supposed to do (requirements). Assign each function to the specific contacts involved. The tool automatically generates a structure chart (network diagram) in the "Visualize" tab to support structure and function analysis (drag nodes to rearrange).
  4. Step 4 – Failure Analysis: Click "Generate FMEA Analysis". A complete FMEA table will be created. For each function, add failure modes, effects, causes and current controls.
  5. Step 5 – Risk Analysis: Assign Severity (S), Occurrence (O) and Detection (D) scores (1‑10) using the dropdowns. The tool calculates:
    • RPN = S × O × D (color‑coded: red ≥150, orange ≥80).
    • Action Priority (AP) (High / Medium / Low) according to AIAG‑VDA tables.
  6. Step 6 – Optimization (Post‑Action Risk): For high‑priority risks (AP = H or M), define recommended actions in the "Recommended Actions" column. For each action you can specify the action description, responsible person, and due date. Then, re‑evaluate the risk after implementing those actions using the S2, O2, D2 dropdowns. The tool automatically computes RPN2 and a new AP2, showing the expected risk reduction.
  7. Step 7 – Results Documentation: Export any FMEA analysis to Excel using the "Export Analysis #X to Excel" button. You can also visualize the risk distribution in the Risk Heatmap tab (Severity vs Occurrence) and save the structure chart as JPG.

💡 Tip: The tool allows you to generate multiple FMEA analyses (e.g., for different design iterations or process changes). Each analysis is independent and includes its own set of failure modes, actions, and post‑action risk scores. Use the "Load Example" button to see a complete DFMEA example with pre‑filled components, contacts, functions and planning data.
